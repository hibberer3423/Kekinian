Young People Regard Quality of Democracy in Indonesia as Poor

Liputan6.com, Jakarta AnekaAhoaks and the use of religious issues for politics make young people in urban areas consider that the quality of democracy in Indonesia is poor.


A survey from the Atma Jaya Institute of Public Policy together with the Faculty of Psychology and the Faculty of Business Administration and Communication Science at Atma Jaya University, Jakarta revealed this.

In an official statement received by Health Liputan6.com on Friday (1/3/2019), the study focused on millennial and generation Z perceptions of democracy and politics, the media, and readiness in dealing with demographic bonuses. As a result, 65 percent (914) of respondents considered the quality of Indonesian democracy to be quite poor (762 respondents) and very bad (152 respondents).


The researchers found that the politicization of religious issues was the biggest reason why democracy in Indonesia was so bad (45 percent). In addition, several other reasons are hoaks (22 percent), corruption (17 percent), and radicalism (11 percent).

The interesting thing is, even though they consider the quality of democracy in Indonesia poor, 72 percent of respondents said that they would still vote in the presidential election this year. In addition, 1,136 respondents said they would vote in the 2019 legislative elections.


According to the researchers, this shows that there is optimism to realize rational Indonesian politics and democracy.


These results also indicate that anyone who will become president in the coming year should see young people as agents of change for a more dignified politics," said UAJ Faculty of Psychology lecturer, Indro Adinugraha.


The research itself involved 1,388 young people in Jakarta, Bandung and Surabaya. They get questions in the form of a questionnaire divided into multiple choices, short entries, and attitude scales.


Based on the year of his birth, participants born in 1998 became the largest with a number of 242 respondents. Followed by 219 participants born between 1996 and 1998.
